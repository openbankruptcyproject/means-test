Presumed Abuse — The Formula Calculation

Section 707(b)(2) creates a formula-based calculation for determining whether Chapter 7 filing is presumptively abusive. Above-median debtors must complete this calculation; the result determines whether the case can proceed in Chapter 7 or whether the debtor must convert to Chapter 13.

The formula structure

The presumed-abuse calculation, summarized:

  1. Start with current monthly income (CMI) — 6-month income average, annualized
  2. Subtract IRS allowable expense standards (food, housing, transportation, healthcare, etc.) for the debtor's region and household size
  3. Subtract actual expenses for taxes, mandatory payroll deductions, term life insurance, court-ordered payments, education for disability/employment, healthcare in excess of IRS standards, and similar specific categories
  4. Subtract priority debt payments (taxes, child support, alimony) projected over 60 months
  5. Subtract secured debt arrears and post-petition secured payments projected over 60 months
  6. The result is "monthly disposable income" — multiply by 60 to get a 60-month projection

The presumption thresholds

Presumed abuse arises if:

  • Monthly disposable income times 60 is at least $10,000 (the high-end automatic-presumption threshold), OR
  • Monthly disposable income is between $130 and $234 (per current adjustments) AND the disposable income would pay at least 25% of unsecured non-priority debt over 60 months

If neither threshold is met, presumed abuse does not arise and the debtor passes the means test.

The IRS allowable expenses

The means test relies heavily on IRS Local Standards (housing/utilities, transportation) and IRS National Standards (food, clothing, healthcare). Some critical points:

  • National standards are uniform across the country and depend on household size
  • Local standards vary by county for housing and by region for transportation
  • Healthcare uses national standard plus actual healthcare expense in excess
  • IRS standards are updated annually

Rebutting the presumption

Even where the formula produces presumed abuse, § 707(b)(2)(B) allows the debtor to rebut by showing "special circumstances" justifying additional expenses or income adjustments not captured by the formula. Special circumstances must be:

  • Adequately documented
  • Necessary and reasonable
  • Not otherwise reflected in the calculation

Common rebuttal grounds: serious medical conditions, court-ordered payments not yet adjudicated, business closures with continuing severance obligations, and similar.
