Median Income Figures — By State and Family Size

Current per-state median income figures used to determine whether a Chapter 7 means-test calculation is required. Below median, presumed abuse generally does not apply. Above median, the formula calculation begins.

Current vs. archived figures

Median income figures are revised approximately every six months by the U.S. Trustee Program. The numbers shown here track the most recent published figures. Always verify against the current UST publication for any specific case — a few thousand dollars in either direction can determine whether a debtor is above or below the threshold.

How median income works in practice

The means test compares the debtor's current monthly income (CMI), annualized, to the median income for the debtor's family size in their state. CMI is the average of the debtor's income for the 6 months preceding the filing.

Family size includes the debtor, the debtor's spouse, and the debtor's dependents. There is occasional ambiguity around adult children, recent additions to the household, etc., but the general rule is that anyone the debtor reasonably claims as a dependent counts.

Current published medians (general framework)

Median income figures vary substantially by state. The general pattern:

  • High-income states (Hawaii, California, Massachusetts, Maryland, New Jersey, Washington) — medians for a household of 4 typically run $110,000-$140,000+
  • Mid-income states (most of the country) — medians for a household of 4 typically run $80,000-$110,000
  • Lower-income states (Mississippi, West Virginia, Alabama, Arkansas) — medians for a household of 4 typically run $65,000-$85,000

For one-person households, medians are roughly half. For two-person households, roughly two-thirds. The full table by state and family size is available on the UST Program's site and is incorporated into Form 122A (Chapter 7 Means Test).

Why the comparison matters

If the debtor's annualized CMI is below the median for the debtor's state and family size, presumed abuse does not apply (per § 707(b)(7)). The debtor is generally eligible for Chapter 7 without needing to engage with the formula calculation.

If above median, the debtor must complete the full presumed-abuse calculation (Form 122A-2). The calculation may still result in passing the test, but it requires going through the IRS allowable expense standards and applying secured-debt and priority-debt deductions.
