The Chapter 7 Means Test

The means test under 11 U.S.C. § 707(b) is the gateway to Chapter 7 relief for consumer debtors. This research project provides a plain-language educational reference on the test's structure, the per-state median income figures, presumed-abuse calculations, and the § 707(b)(3) totality-of-circumstances test.

The two-part test

Section 707(b) creates a two-step framework for evaluating whether a Chapter 7 case constitutes "abuse" by a consumer debtor:

  • Step 1 — presumed abuse (§ 707(b)(2)): a formula-based calculation using the debtor's current monthly income and IRS allowable expense standards. If the formula produces a result above specified thresholds, abuse is presumed.
  • Step 2 — totality of circumstances (§ 707(b)(3)): a discretionary inquiry into the totality of the debtor's financial circumstances. Even if presumed abuse doesn't apply, a court can find abuse based on bad faith or other circumstances.

The income threshold

Below the per-state median income for the debtor's family size, the means test essentially does not apply — the debtor passes automatically. Above the median, the formula calculation begins.

Median income figures are published periodically by the U.S. Trustee Program based on Census data and are updated typically twice per year (spring and fall). The current figures are available on the UST Program website and on this research project's data page.

What the means test is and isn't

The means test is NOT the only consideration in Chapter 7 eligibility. Other rules apply: the § 109 eligibility provisions, § 101 definitions, the § 1328(f) lookback if the debtor recently received a discharge in another case, and the § 707(a) and (b) abuse provisions. The means test is the dominant Ch.7 gating function for consumers, but it operates within this broader framework.
