Pre-Bankruptcy Planning Around the Means Test

Pre-bankruptcy planning to legitimately satisfy the means test is an important practice area. The line between proper planning and improper manipulation is well-defined; this page covers the framework. Educational only; specific planning requires a licensed bankruptcy attorney.

Permissible planning

Some pre-bankruptcy actions are clearly within the bounds of legitimate planning:

  • Curing IRS allowable expenses: if the debtor has been spending below IRS housing standards, ensuring household expenses align with normal IRS allowable amounts before filing
  • Income timing: filing during a 6-month period when CMI accurately reflects current circumstances rather than a recent spike
  • Funding retirement accounts: contributions to qualified retirement plans are generally permissible up to limits
  • Paying secured debt arrearages: within reason, bringing secured debts current pre-petition
  • Education for disability or employment: reasonable expenditures count as permissible expense categories

Impermissible manipulation

Other pre-bankruptcy actions cross into manipulation that can support § 707(b)(3) bad-faith findings:

  • Quitting a job to lower CMI: deliberate income reduction to manipulate the test
  • Transferring assets to non-debtor relatives: particularly suspect within 90 days of filing
  • Inflating debts: taking on additional credit-card debt with intent to discharge
  • Concealing income sources: contracting through related entities to hide CMI
  • Sudden expense spikes: luxury purchases or large discretionary spending on the eve of filing

The 90-day window

The Bankruptcy Code's avoidance powers (§ 547 preferences, § 548 fraudulent transfers) reach back at least 90 days for non-insider preferences and 1 year for fraudulent transfers and insider preferences. Pre-petition transfers within these windows can be unwound by the trustee.

Tax planning around bankruptcy

Tax issues interact with the means test:

  • Tax debt: priority tax debt counts against the debtor in the means-test calculation but is not dischargeable in Ch.7 anyway
  • Tax refund timing: refunds received within 6 months pre-petition are part of CMI
  • Withholding adjustments: bringing W-4 withholding to actual liability avoids inflating CMI from refund timing
