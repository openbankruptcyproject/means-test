Totality of the Circumstances — § 707(b)(3)

Even where presumed abuse under § 707(b)(2) does not apply, § 707(b)(3) gives the U.S. Trustee or the court the authority to find abuse based on the totality of the debtor's financial circumstances. This is the discretionary backstop to the formula-based test.

The two prongs

Section 707(b)(3) lists two grounds for finding abuse:

  • (b)(3)(A) bad faith: filing was undertaken in bad faith
  • (b)(3)(B) totality of the financial circumstances: the totality of the debtor's financial circumstances demonstrates abuse

Bad faith filing — (b)(3)(A)

Bad faith analysis turns on the totality of the debtor's conduct. Common factors courts consider:

  • Concealment of assets or income on the schedules
  • Inflated or fictitious debts to qualify for Chapter 7
  • Recent luxury purchases or large transfers to family members
  • Pre-bankruptcy income spike followed by petition filing
  • Repeated filings designed to frustrate creditors
  • Misleading disclosures to the trustee or court

Totality of the financial circumstances — (b)(3)(B)

This prong asks whether the debtor's overall financial picture — even after passing the formula test — nonetheless reflects an ability to pay creditors meaningfully. Factors:

  • Whether the debtor enjoys a relatively high standard of living that would not be substantially impaired by some Chapter 13 plan
  • Whether the debtor's actual expenses (above IRS standards) reflect genuine need or discretionary preferences
  • Whether the debtor has stable, predictable above-median income that could fund a Chapter 13 plan
  • Whether the debt burden is large relative to the debtor's earning capacity

Practical effect

The U.S. Trustee Program identifies cases for § 707(b)(3) review based on patterns: high CMI, sophisticated debt structures, recent income spikes, large discretionary expenditures, and similar markers. UST motions to dismiss or convert under § 707(b)(3) are not common, but they do occur and can result in conversion to Chapter 13.

The In re Lapczynski / Crage line

Several circuit-level decisions have addressed how broadly § 707(b)(3)(B) reads. The trend is toward giving courts substantial discretion, but with the limit that bare ability-to-pay alone is not enough — there must be some additional factor (lifestyle, conduct, structure of debt) suggesting that Chapter 7 relief is not warranted.
